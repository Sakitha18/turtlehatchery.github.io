Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery

Preserving and Protecting Endangered Turtles

Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery, located in the southern coastal region of Sri Lanka, is a non-profit organization that focuses on protecting and conserving the country's turtle population. Established in 1981, the hatchery has been instrumental in preserving the endangered species of turtles by creating a safe environment for their nesting and hatching.

History - Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery

Dudley Perera initiated the Kosgoda Sea Turtle Conservation Project in 1981. The project's primary goal is to preserve sea turtles and safeguard them against predators like birds, dogs, and crabs. It is one of the 18 hatcheries in Sri Lanka specialized to raising this threatened species properly.

The project's main objective is to protect the endangered sea turtle species by providing a safe and secure environment for them to nest, hatch, and grow. The staff of the Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery collect the turtle eggs from the beach at night to protect them from predators and poachers. Once the eggs are collected, they are taken to the hatchery and placed in sand-filled pits to incubate. When the eggs hatch, the baby turtles are kept in tanks until they are strong enough to be released into the sea. The hatchery is also responsible for raising injured turtles until they are well enough to be released back into the wild.

In addition to its conservation efforts, the Kosgoda Sea Turtle Conservation Project also educates tourists and locals about the importance of sea turtle conservation. Visitors can observe the hatchery's daily operations and learn about the different types of sea turtles found in Sri Lanka. The project also offers turtle-watching tours, where visitors can observe the turtles in their natural habitat. Overall, the Kosgoda Sea Turtle Conservation Project plays a crucial role in preserving Sri Lanka's sea turtle population and raising awareness about the importance of conservation.

Available Turtles

Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery is home to five of the seven species of sea turtles found in the world.

OLIVE RIDLEY SEA TURTLE

The green turtle is the most commonly encountered turtle in Sri Lanka. It can be found feeding on seagrass beds and algae in shallow waters. Green turtles are endangered.

GREEN SEA TURTLE

Loggerhead turtles are relatively rare in Sri Lanka, but they can be found in the waters around the island. They are critically endangered and face a variety of threats, including habitat loss, hunting, and pollution.

LOGGERHEAD SEA TURTLE

They are typically found in coral reef habitats and feed on sponges and other invertebrates. In Sri Lanka, hawksbill turtles are particularly vulnerable to habitat loss, hunting, and the illegal trade in their shells.

HAWKSBILL SEA TURTLE

Olive Ridley turtles are one of the smallest sea turtle species and can be found in large numbers along Sri Lanka's coast. They are listed as vulnerable, and their populations are declining due to habitat loss, hunting.

LEATHERBACK SEA TURTLE

Leatherback turtles are the largest sea turtle species and can grow up to 2 meters in length. In Sri Lanka, leatherback turtles are particularly vulnerable to coastal development and the destruction of nesting sites.
